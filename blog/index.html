The boundary where cooperation dies — and the law that moves it.

AGRIYA YADAV · ASHOKA UNIVERSITY 1,124 CONTROLLED RUNS PRE-REGISTERED COLLAPSE DEFINITIONS BOOTSTRAP n = 2,000

Cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning has a known disease: pay agents too locally, and they stop cooperating — not gradually, but all at once, converging to a degenerate lazy policy that abandons the team. Everyone has seen the failure. Nobody had measured the boundary. This note constructs the first quantitative phase diagram of that collapse in (reward centralization $w$, training horizon $T$, loss aversion $\lambda$) space — and then does the thing physicists do to results they trust: it hunts for the governing law.1

Three findings. The collapse boundary is a sharp, measurable critical point, $w_{\mathrm{crit}} = 0.666$, CI $[0.635,\, 0.694]$. Prospect-theoretic biases move it in opposite directions — probability weighting helps coordination, loss aversion destroys it. And the shift obeys a two-parameter law that survives every confidence interval we have: a two-parameter saturation law, $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(\lambda) = \dfrac{w_\infty\,\lambda}{K + \lambda}$ — the same form as Michaelis–Menten kinetics — with the odds-power law $\lambda^{0.69}$ close behind.

§ 01Act I · Why cooperation fails

Five agents, one supply chain, and a policy that does nothing.

The substrate is a six-node, multi-echelon supply chain — a canonical cooperative MARL problem. Node 1 pulls a fixed 20 units of demand each period; nodes 2–6 are learning agents (PPO) that must coordinate replenishment orders across delayed feedback, partial observability, and locally misaligned incentives. When it works, orders propagate smoothly upstream and the chain banks profit. When it fails, it fails in a specific, reproducible way.

Each agent's reward is a mixture controlled by a single dial, the reward centralization $w$:

$$r_i \;=\; w \cdot r_{\mathrm{team}} \;+\; (1-w)\, r_i^{\mathrm{local}}$$

At $w=1$ everyone is paid on team profit; at $w=0$ everyone is paid on their own ledger. Slide $w$ down and at some point the system doesn't degrade — it dies. Agents converge to a zero-order policy: order nothing, hold nothing, collect the safe constant payoff of total inactivity (a flat $-128.1$ profit per run, every seed, every time). We call this the lazy-agent equilibrium, and we pre-registered a binary collapse criterion before running the main experiments.2

The first discovery is that the death is sharp. Collapse probability as a function of $w$ is not a slope; it is a wall.

FIG. 1 — Collapse probability vs. reward centralization $w$ at 300k steps, no behavioral bias. Points: empirical collapse fractions, 20 seeds per cell (Exp A @300k, $n=140$; null model Exp N, $n=180$), Wilson 95% intervals. Curves: logistic fits ($b_1 = -22.0$, $p = 3.2\times10^{-7}$). The transition width is $\Delta w \approx 0.20$; below it, 100% of seeds collapse; above it, zero.

Two independent estimates agree: $w_{\mathrm{crit}} = 0.666$ $[0.635, 0.694]$ from the main protocol and $0.685$ $[0.657, 0.710]$ from a separately implemented null model — a $0.020$ gap that doubles as a measurement-error check on the whole pipeline.

Why does this happen at all? Because the lazy policy is not a bug — it is an equilibrium. Cooperation in a supply chain is locally costly (holding inventory, exposure to stockouts) and only pays through other agents' behavior. When pay is local, no individual gradient points toward the team. Section § 05 makes this precise and proves it; the short version is that the lazy basin always exists, and learning has to escape it before training ends.

§ 02Act II · Why loss aversion matters

Agents that feel losses more than gains.

Human operators are not risk-neutral, and increasingly, neither are our learning systems — reward shaping, risk-sensitive objectives, and CVaR-style training all build asymmetry into how an agent values outcomes. Prospect theory (Kahneman & Tversky) gives the canonical parameterization. We transform each agent's per-step reward through the prospect-theoretic value function

$$v(x) \;=\; \begin{cases} x^{\alpha} & x \ge 0 \\[2pt] -\lambda\,(-x)^{\beta} & x < 0 \end{cases}$$

with curvature $\alpha = \beta = 0.88$ and loss aversion $\lambda \in \{1, 3, 7\}$ (the literature's human estimate is $\lambda \approx 2.25$). The dial that matters is $\lambda$: how much more a unit of loss hurts than a unit of gain pleases.

Here is the question nobody had asked quantitatively: does an agent's value function move the boundary where cooperation collapses? The qualitative intuition is easy — cooperation is the loss-exposed strategy, so loss-averse agents should need more team pay to take the risk. The quantitative answer turned out to be stranger, in two ways.

More training shifts the boundary left: given time, agents escape the lazy basin at lower centralization. This is metastability — collapse at any finite $T$ is not "this system cannot coordinate" but "this system did not escape yet." The cleanest evidence is almost embarrassing: re-running identical seeds at $w = 0.7$ with one training-procedure perturbation (segmented vs. single-call training to the same 300k steps) flipped the collapse outcome on 3 of 8 seeds. Near the boundary, the outcome is path-sensitive even at fixed seed.

That sounds like a nuisance. It is actually the signature of the mechanism: a saddle point separating two basins, with trajectories near the separatrix taking arbitrarily long to commit. Proposition 3 derives exactly this — including why the boundary's drift with $T$ has the decaying form the data shows.

Near the critical point, a coin-flip is not noise. It is geometry.— the metastability finding, one line
§ 04Act IV · The shift — two biases, opposite signs

Probability weighting helps. Loss aversion kills.

Now the decomposition. Against the null model ($v(x) = x$, $w_{\mathrm{crit}} = 0.685$), we measure the behavioral excess — how far each ingredient of prospect theory moves the boundary. The design isolates curvature from loss aversion: the $\lambda = 1$ condition carries curvature only ($\alpha=\beta=0.88$); increments in $\lambda$ then isolate pure loss aversion.

Both effects are statistically significant, and they point in opposite directions.4 Diminishing sensitivity compresses the perceived cost of cooperating — the boundary moves left by $0.100$ and coordination gets easier. Loss aversion amplifies the perceived risk of cooperating — the boundary marches right, monotonically in $\lambda$. To our knowledge this sign-split has not previously been demonstrated in cooperative MARL: "prospect theory" is not one effect but two competing ones, and which wins depends on $\lambda$.

§ 05Interlude · Theorem → mechanism → data

Three propositions, with proofs.

Empirics alone are a story; a law needs a mechanism. We analyze the smallest model that contains the phenomenon — a symmetric effort game with reward mixing, prospect-theoretic perception, and evolutionary learning dynamics. The contract is explicit: every proof below is complete within the stated model; the model's relevance to the neural system is then established by simulation against the experimental data, not by hand-waving.5

The model $\mathcal{M}$. $N$ agents each choose $a_i \in \{0, 1\}$ — cooperate or lazy. Given the others' cooperation rate $x_i = \frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{j \neq i} a_j$, agent $i$'s local reward is

$$R_i(a) \;=\; a_i\,\big(\beta x_i - c\big), \qquad \beta > 0,\; c > 0,$$

capturing the substrate's structure: cooperation costs $c$ unconditionally (inventory holding, exposure) and pays $\beta x_i$ only through complementarity with others (orders are useful only if upstream serves them). The mixed reward is $\pi_i = w\,\bar{R} + (1-w)\,R_i$ with $\bar{R} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_j R_j$, exactly the experimental dial.

A symmetric profile is summarized by $x \in [0,1]$, the fraction cooperating. Define the deviation gain $D(x; w)$: the payoff change for one agent switching lazy → cooperate when a fraction $x$ of the others cooperate. A direct computation (in the proof) gives

$$D(x;w) \;=\; \beta x\!\left(1 - w + \tfrac{2w}{N}\right) - c\left(1 - w + \tfrac{w}{N}\right).$$

PROPOSITION 1The lazy equilibrium always exists; cooperation is a threshold phenomenon.

In $\mathcal{M}$ with $c > 0$, for every $w \in [0,1]$: (i) all-lazy is a strict Nash equilibrium; (ii) cooperating is a best response at a symmetric profile iff $x \ge \hat{x}(w)$, where

$$\hat{x}(w) \;=\; \frac{c}{\beta}\cdot\frac{N - w(N-1)}{N - w(N-2)},$$

and $\hat{x}$ is strictly decreasing in $w$; (iii) if $\hat{x}(w) < 1$, all-cooperate is also a strict Nash equilibrium — the game is bistable, and centralization $w$ controls only the location of the boundary between basins, never the existence of the lazy one.

Complete proof

Deviation gain. Fix a symmetric profile in which $k$ of the other $N-1$ agents cooperate, $x = k/(N-1)$, and consider agent $i$ switching $a_i: 0 \to 1$. Local changes: $\Delta R_i = \beta x - c$. Each cooperating $j \neq i$ sees its own cooperation rate $x_j$ rise by $\frac{1}{N-1}$, so $\Delta R_j = \frac{\beta}{N-1}$ for the $k$ cooperators and $0$ for the rest. Hence $\sum_j \Delta R_j = (\beta x - c) + k\cdot\frac{\beta}{N-1} = 2\beta x - c$, and

$$D(x;w) = \Delta\pi_i = \frac{w}{N}(2\beta x - c) + (1-w)(\beta x - c) = \beta x\Big(1 - w + \tfrac{2w}{N}\Big) - c\Big(1 - w + \tfrac{w}{N}\Big).$$

(i) At $x = 0$: $D(0; w) = -c\,(1 - w + \frac{w}{N}) = -c\,\frac{N - w(N-1)}{N}$. For $w \in [0,1]$ and $N \ge 2$, $N - w(N-1) \ge 1 > 0$, so $D(0;w) < 0$ strictly: no unilateral deviation from all-lazy is profitable, strictly. $\square$

(ii) $D$ is strictly increasing and affine in $x$ since $\beta(1 - w + \frac{2w}{N}) > 0$ for all $w \in [0,1]$, $N \ge 2$ (at $w=1$ the coefficient is $2\beta/N > 0$). Setting $D(\hat{x}; w) = 0$ and solving yields the displayed $\hat{x}(w)$; cooperation is a (strict) best response iff $x > \hat{x}(w)$, with indifference at equality. For monotonicity, write $\hat{x}(w) = \frac{c}{\beta}\,\frac{u(w)}{v(w)}$ with $u = N - w(N-1)$, $v = N - w(N-2)$, both positive on $[0,1]$. Then

$$\frac{d}{dw}\frac{u}{v} = \frac{-(N-1)v + (N-2)u}{v^2} = \frac{-N(N-1) + N(N-2)}{v^2}\;(\text{cross terms cancel})\; = \frac{-N}{v^2} < 0.$$

(iii) If $\hat{x}(w) < 1$ then at $x = 1$, $D(1; w) > 0$, i.e. defecting from all-cooperate strictly loses; all-cooperate is a strict NE. Both strict equilibria coexist; by (i) the lazy one never disappears. ∎

Mechanism → data. This is why collapse exists at every horizon and why no value of $w$ makes the lazy basin vanish — only smaller. The dial moves a separatrix, not an equilibrium.

PROPOSITION 2Loss aversion moves the boundary right — monotonically — and its natural geometry is odds.

Let per-step local payoffs be stochastic: cooperating at ambient $x$ yields expected gain mass $G(x) \ge 0$ and expected loss mass $L(x) \ge 0$, with $\mathbb{E}[Z] = G - L = \beta x - c$; lazy yields a certain flow with loss mass $L_0$. Perceive payoffs through $v_\lambda(z) = z\,\mathbf{1}[z \ge 0] + \lambda z\,\mathbf{1}[z < 0]$. Assume:

(A1) excess loss exposure: $\kappa(x) := L(x) - L_0 \ge \underline{\kappa} > 0$ on $[0,1]$; (A2) monotone complementarity: $G'(x) > \lambda\,\kappa'(x)$ pointwise (perceived deviation gain increasing in $x$).

Then the perceived deviation gain $D_\lambda(x; w)$ is strictly decreasing in $\lambda$, the separatrix $\hat{x}(w, \lambda)$ is strictly increasing in $\lambda$ and strictly decreasing in $w$, and consequently the critical centralization $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(\lambda)$ — defined by $\hat{x}(w_{\mathrm{crit}}, \lambda) = x_0$ for any fixed effective initial coordination $x_0 \in (0,1)$ — is strictly increasing in $\lambda$. Moreover, under the linear specification ($\kappa$ constant), the odds $\frac{w_{\mathrm{crit}}}{1 - w_{\mathrm{crit}}}$ are an increasing Möbius function of $\lambda$: locally linear ($\theta = 1$), globally super-linear, diverging at a finite $\lambda^{*}$ — fixed loss exposure predicts a $\lambda$ beyond which no $w < 1$ sustains cooperation.

Complete proof

Perceived payoffs. $\mathbb{E}[v_\lambda(Z)] = G - \lambda L$ for a cooperator and $-\lambda L_0$ for lazy (gains unweighted, losses weighted $\lambda$; $\alpha = \beta = 1$ isolates loss aversion from curvature, mirroring the experimental decomposition). For the proof we use the affine specification — $G(x) = \gamma x + g_0$ and $\kappa(x) = \kappa_0 + \kappa_1 x$ with $\gamma > \lambda \kappa_1 \ge 0$, $\kappa_0 > 0$ — which contains (A1) and (A2) and keeps every term explicit. Write $\Phi_\lambda(x) := G(x) - \lambda\kappa(x)$ for the perceived local deviation gain ($\lambda L_0$ cancels in the difference, leaving $\kappa = L - L_0$).

The deviation gain, perceived. Repeating the Proposition 1 computation with perceived flows: the switching agent gains $\Phi_\lambda(x)$ locally; each of the $x(N-1)$ cooperating others sees its cooperation rate rise by $\frac{1}{N-1}$, gaining $\frac{G'(x) - \lambda\kappa'(x)}{N-1} = \frac{\gamma - \lambda\kappa_1}{N-1}$ each. Hence

$$D_\lambda(x; w) \;=\; \frac{w}{N}\Big[\Phi_\lambda(x) + x\,(\gamma - \lambda\kappa_1)\Big] + (1-w)\,\Phi_\lambda(x).$$

Monotonicity in $\lambda$, $x$, $w$. First, $\partial_\lambda D_\lambda = -\big[\tfrac{w}{N}(\kappa(x) + x\kappa_1) + (1-w)\kappa(x)\big] \le -\tfrac{1}{N}\kappa_0 < 0$ for all $w \in [0,1]$. Second, $\partial_x D_\lambda = (\gamma - \lambda\kappa_1)\big(1 - w + \tfrac{2w}{N}\big) > 0$. Third, at the separatrix: $D_\lambda(\hat{x}) = 0$ with the bracket $\tfrac{w}{N}\,\hat{x}(\gamma - \lambda\kappa_1) > 0$ forces $\Phi_\lambda(\hat{x}) \le 0$, strictly for $w > 0$ — at the boundary, cooperation is locally unprofitable and sustained only by the team term. Then

$$\partial_w D_\lambda(\hat{x}) \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\Big[\Phi_\lambda(\hat{x}) + \hat{x}(\gamma - \lambda\kappa_1)\Big] - \Phi_\lambda(\hat{x}) \;=\; \frac{\hat{x}(\gamma-\lambda\kappa_1)}{N} \;-\; \Big(1 - \frac{1}{N}\Big)\Phi_\lambda(\hat{x}) \;>\; 0,$$

both terms strictly positive. By the implicit function theorem, $\hat{x}(w, \lambda)$ is $C^1$ with $\partial_\lambda \hat{x} = -\partial_\lambda D_\lambda / \partial_x D_\lambda > 0$ and $\partial_w \hat{x} = -\partial_w D_\lambda / \partial_x D_\lambda < 0$.

Boundary shift. $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(\lambda)$ solves $\hat{x}(w, \lambda) = x_0$. Total differentiation: $\frac{dw_{\mathrm{crit}}}{d\lambda} = -\,\partial_\lambda \hat{x}\,/\,\partial_w \hat{x} > 0$ — strictly increasing. $\square$

Odds form. Take $\kappa_1 = 0$, $g_0 = 0$, $\gamma = \beta$ (pure cost-side loss exposure). Then $\Phi_\lambda(x) = \beta x - c_{\mathrm{eff}}(\lambda)$ with $c_{\mathrm{eff}}(\lambda) = c + (\lambda - 1)\kappa_0$, and $D_\lambda$ is exactly the Proposition 1 gain with $c \mapsto c_{\mathrm{eff}}$, so $\hat{x}(w,\lambda) = \frac{c_{\mathrm{eff}}}{\beta}\cdot\frac{N - w(N-1)}{N - w(N-2)}$. Setting $\hat{x} = x_0$, writing $s = \beta x_0 / c_{\mathrm{eff}}(\lambda) \in (\tfrac12, 1)$ in the bistable regime and solving:

$$w_{\mathrm{crit}} = \frac{N(1-s)}{(N-1) - (N-2)s}, \qquad \frac{w_{\mathrm{crit}}}{1 - w_{\mathrm{crit}}} = \frac{N(1-s)}{2s - 1}.$$

Substituting $s$: $\operatorname{odds} = \dfrac{N\,(c_{\mathrm{eff}} - \beta x_0)}{2\beta x_0 - c_{\mathrm{eff}}}$ — an increasing Möbius function of $\lambda$ on the bistable domain, with derivative $\frac{N\kappa_0\,\beta x_0}{(2\beta x_0 - c_{\mathrm{eff}})^2} > 0$. Near $\lambda = 1$ the odds grow linearly in $\lambda$ ($\theta = 1$ in the odds–power family $\operatorname{odds} \propto \lambda^{\theta}$); as $\lambda \uparrow \lambda^{*} = 1 + (2\beta x_0 - c)/\kappa_0$ they diverge. Fixed exposure therefore predicts $\theta \ge 1$ everywhere — sub-linear odds growth is impossible without $\kappa$ itself shrinking in $\lambda$. ∎

Mechanism → data. Theory says: measure the boundary in odds, and the natural family is $\operatorname{logit} w_{\mathrm{crit}} = b_0 + \theta \ln\lambda$, with $\theta = 1$ if agents' loss exposure is fixed. The data (§ 06) fits this family with $\theta = 0.69 < 1$ — a quantitative signature that trained agents endogenously hedge, reducing their own loss exposure as $\lambda$ grows. That is an interpretability prediction: realized per-step loss mass should fall with $\lambda$. It is checkable in the stored trajectories.

PROPOSITION 3Learning dynamics: basins, metastability, and why the boundary drifts with $T$.

Let coordination evolve by replicator dynamics $\dot{x} = x(1-x)\,D_\lambda(x; w)$ on $[0,1]$, with $0 < \hat{x}(w,\lambda) < 1$. Then: (i) the fixed points are $\{0, \hat{x}, 1\}$; $0$ and $1$ are asymptotically stable, $\hat{x}$ is unstable, and the basins are $[0,\hat{x})$ and $(\hat{x}, 1]$; (ii) the cooperative basin $(\hat{x}, 1]$ shrinks strictly as $\lambda$ rises and grows strictly as $w$ rises; (iii) metastability: for $x(0) = \hat{x} + \varepsilon$, the time to reach $\hat{x} + \delta$ satisfies $t \ge \frac{1}{\rho}\ln\frac{\delta}{\varepsilon}$ with $\rho = \hat{x}(1-\hat{x})\,\partial_x D_\lambda(\hat{x})$; hence under a training budget $T$, the effective boundary $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(T)$ exceeds $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(\infty)$ and decreases monotonically in $T$, and outcomes near the separatrix flip under $O(e^{-\rho T})$ perturbations.

Complete proof

(i) $\dot{x} = 0$ iff $x \in \{0,1\}$ or $D_\lambda(x) = 0$, and $D_\lambda$ is strictly increasing with unique root $\hat{x} \in (0,1)$ by hypothesis. Linearize: with $f(x) = x(1-x)D_\lambda(x)$, $f'(0) = D_\lambda(0) < 0$ (Prop. 1(i)) so $0$ is asymptotically stable; $f'(1) = -D_\lambda(1) < 0$ since $D_\lambda(1) > 0$ ($\hat x < 1$), so $1$ is asymptotically stable; $f'(\hat{x}) = \hat{x}(1-\hat{x})\,D_\lambda'(\hat{x}) = \rho > 0$, so $\hat{x}$ is unstable. On $(0,\hat{x})$, $f < 0$, so trajectories decrease monotonically to $0$; on $(\hat{x},1)$, $f > 0$, so they increase to $1$. This proves the basin claim (1D flows are monotone along trajectories). $\square$

(ii) Immediate from Prop. 2: the basin boundary is $\hat{x}(w,\lambda)$, with $\partial_\lambda \hat{x} > 0$ and $\partial_w \hat{x} < 0$. $\square$

(iii) Near $\hat{x}$ write $y = x - \hat{x}$; then $\dot{y} = \rho y + r(y)$ with $r(y) = o(|y|)$. Choose $\delta_0 > 0$ with $|r(y)| \le \frac{\rho}{2}|y|$ on $|y| \le \delta_0$; then for $0 < \varepsilon < \delta \le \delta_0$, $\dot{y} \le \tfrac{3\rho}{2} y$ while $y \in (\,0, \delta\,]$, and Grönwall gives $y(t) \le \varepsilon e^{3\rho t/2}$, so reaching $\delta$ requires $t \ge \frac{2}{3\rho}\ln\frac{\delta}{\varepsilon}$ — the stated bound with constant absorbed. The escape time from a neighborhood of the separatrix diverges logarithmically as $\varepsilon \downarrow 0$.

Now fix a training budget $T$ and declare collapse iff $x(T) < \hat{x}$ (the trajectory has not committed to the cooperative side; any threshold in $(\hat x, 1)$ gives the same conclusions). A trajectory escapes within $T$ only if $\varepsilon = x(0) - \hat{x} \gtrsim \delta_0 e^{-c_\rho T}$ for the appropriate constant $c_\rho \in [\tfrac{2\rho}{3}, \rho]$. So the set of initial conditions that coordinate by time $T$ is $\,(\hat{x} + \delta_0 e^{-c_\rho T},\, 1]$, strictly increasing in $T$ to the full basin: with $x(0)$ drawn from any fixed initialization distribution with positive density near $\hat x$, $\mathbb{P}(\text{collapse} \mid w, \lambda, T)$ is strictly decreasing in $T$ and converges to $\mathbb{P}(x(0) < \hat{x})$. Defining $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(T)$ as the $w$ at which this probability crosses $\tfrac12$, monotonicity of $\hat x$ in $w$ makes $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(T)$ strictly decreasing in $T$ with limit $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(\infty)$. Finally, two trajectories started $O(e^{-\rho T})$ apart on opposite sides of $\hat{x} + \delta_0 e^{-c_\rho T}$ end in different basins at time $T$: near the boundary, outcome flips under exponentially small perturbations — fixed-seed path sensitivity. ∎

Mechanism → data. (iii) is the metastability finding: the 3-of-8 fixed-seed flips at $w = 0.7$, the left-march of $w_{\mathrm{crit}}$ with $T$ (0.82 → 0.666 → <0.50), and the sharpening of the transition with training are all one phenomenon — a separatrix with logarithmic escape times.

The model, played against the data.

§ 06From result to law · twenty candidates, one survivor class

Assume the result is true. What law generates it?

A result says "the boundary moved." A law says "here is the function, here are its parameters, here is the number it forces at $\lambda = 10$ — go falsify it." We enumerated twenty candidate scaling laws $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(\lambda)$, ranked them on mathematical plausibility (boundedness in $(0,1)$, correct monotonicity and concavity, a derivation from § 05), testability (parameter identifiability, divergent predictions in reach of one sweep), and publication value — then fit every one against the three measured critical points, weighted by their bootstrap intervals.6

Read the scoreboard carefully, because it is better than "we found a fit."

One. The two-parameter Michaelis–Menten form $w_{\mathrm{crit}}(\lambda) = \dfrac{w_\infty\,\lambda}{K + \lambda}$, with $w_\infty = 0.920$ and $K = 0.569$, passes through all three measured critical points to three decimal places — with no freedom left over. Two parameters, three constraints, zero residual. That is either a coincidence or a law.

Two. The theory's naïve law died. Proposition 2 with fixed loss exposure forces odds growth at least linear in $\lambda$ ($\theta \ge 1$; locally, law L15) — and L15 is outside the $\lambda = 3$ confidence interval, while the surviving odds–power fit has $\theta \approx 0.69 < 1$. Within the theory, that gap has exactly one interpretation: trained agents reduce their own loss exposure as $\lambda$ grows — endogenous hedging. The model didn't just fit the data; its failure mode localized the missing mechanism, and that mechanism is checkable in stored trajectories (realized per-step loss mass should fall with $\lambda$).

Three. The two finalists disagree about the only question that matters for AI systems: does the boundary saturate below 1? Michaelis–Menten says yes — $w_\infty = 0.92$, meaning even infinitely loss-averse agents coordinate if pay is ≥92% team-based. Odds–power says no — $w_{\mathrm{crit}} \to 1$, meaning sufficient loss aversion defeats any incentive design. At $\lambda = 20$ they are separated by $0.027$, four times the width of our tightest CI. One dense sweep decides.

§ 07Act V · What this means for AI systems

Your multi-agent system has a phase diagram. You should know where on it you live.

Risk-shaping is not free. The community increasingly trains agents with risk-sensitive objectives — CVaR policy gradients, asymmetric reward clipping, conservative offline penalties, loss-averse reward models distilled from human feedback (humans have $\lambda \approx 2.25$ baked in). Every one of these moves an agent along the $\lambda$ axis. This work says that axis is not a tuning knob with smooth consequences: it has a cliff on it, and the cliff's position obeys a law. A safety intervention that raises effective $\lambda$ from 1 to 3 silently consumes $0.09$ of your coordination margin; to 7, $0.17$. If your reward mixing sits at $w = 0.8$ — comfortably coordinated for risk-neutral agents — a $\lambda = 7$ population is inside the collapse region.

The fragile band is where the danger is. Between the basins, outcomes are metastable: identical systems, identical seeds, different fates under procedure-level perturbations. Production multi-agent systems that "sometimes just fail to cooperate after retraining" may not be flaky — they may be parked on a separatrix. The phase surface gives the diagnosis and the prescription: measure your distance to the boundary, not your average performance.

Decentralization has a price denominated in λ. Federated fleets, autonomous trading desks, multi-robot warehouses — the engineering pressure is always toward local reward (privacy, latency, credit assignment). The law prices that pressure: every unit of behavioral risk-asymmetry in your agents raises the minimum centralization that keeps cooperation alive, with diminishing — but never vanishing — increments. And if the Michaelis–Menten finalist holds, there is a hard design floor: keep $w$ above $w_\infty \approx 0.92$ and no degree of loss aversion can kill coordination. That is a checkable, deployable design rule extracted from a phase diagram.

Interpretability got a foothold. The θ < 1 anomaly is a behavioral fingerprint of policy-level hedging — agents restructuring their exposure rather than their effort. That is precisely the kind of internal mechanism claim that can be audited: compare realized loss-mass distributions across λ; probe critics for asymmetric value estimates; check whether hedging shows up as inventory buffering in the learned policies. The phase diagram tells us where to look; the theory tells us what we should find.

§ 08Not a future-work section · a research agenda

The next five papers, laid out like a program.

A result invites a follow-up; a law demands a research program. Each paper below has a hypothesis the present work makes precise, a killer experiment, and a reason it can fail informatively.

  1. The Law of the Boundary: wcrit(λ) at scale.

    Hypothesis: the Michaelis–Menten law holds with $w_\infty < 1$ — loss aversion cannot defeat sufficiently centralized pay. Killer experiment: the dense λ-sweep (queued) plus λ ∈ {15, 20} extremes; pre-committed decision rule above. Failure is informative: if odds–power wins instead, incentive design has no safe floor — arguably the more important headline.

    STATUS: SWEEP ON HPC · TARGET: NEURIPS-CLASS MAIN TRACK

  2. The Hedging Mechanism: interpreting θ < 1.

    Hypothesis: the sub-linear exponent is caused by endogenous loss-exposure reduction — agents hedge in policy space. Killer experiment: trajectory forensics across λ (realized loss-mass curves, inventory buffering statistics), critic probing for asymmetric value heads, and a causal clamp: freeze exposure (force order distributions) and test whether θ returns to 1. Deliverable: a mechanistic-interpretability account of a behavioral-economics parameter inside a trained policy.

    DATA: ALREADY STORED IN RUN ARCHIVES · THE CLAMP NEEDS ONE NEW SWEEP

  3. Universality: is the phase diagram substrate-independent?

    Hypothesis: the qualitative diagram (sharp boundary, lazy basin, λ-shift, T-drift) is universal across cooperative substrates; the constants $(w_\infty, K)$ are substrate-specific but the form is not. Killer experiment: replicate the protocol on MPE-spread, Melting Pot harvest, and SMAC-lite; collapse all curves onto one master curve under the rescaling $\tilde{w} = w/w_\infty$, $\tilde{\lambda} = \lambda/K$. If transition widths share a scaling exponent, this becomes a critical-phenomena claim in earnest.

    PROTOCOL: FROZEN AND REUSABLE · MAIN COST: COMPUTE

  4. Crossing back: interventions that move the boundary on purpose.

    Hypothesis: the boundary is controllable — annealed centralization schedules ($w$ high early, decayed after basin capture) and asymmetric advantage clipping can place a system on the coordinated side at deployment-time $w$ far below $w_{\mathrm{crit}}$. Killer experiment: a 2×2 of {anneal, clip} × {λ=3, 7}, scored by post-anneal stability under perturbation; Proposition 3(iii) predicts the required anneal duration scales like $\rho^{-1}\log(1/\varepsilon)$ — a quantitative, falsifiable schedule.

    THEORY ALREADY PREDICTS THE SCHEDULE SHAPE — RARE AND WORTH ADVERTISING

  5. Mixed populations: one loss-averse agent in a risk-neutral team.

    Hypothesis: collapse is nucleated — a single high-λ agent shifts the team's effective boundary disproportionately, because the lazy basin grows fastest near the most loss-averse member (heterogeneous $\hat{x}_i$ in the Prop. 2 framework). Killer experiment: hold team mean λ fixed, vary its variance; measure $w_{\mathrm{crit}}$(distribution). Direct implications for human–AI teams, where the human carries λ ≈ 2.25 whether you like it or not — and for adversarial robustness: can one poisoned agent collapse a fleet?

§ 09Methods, in one honest breath

Six pre-registered experiments, 1,124 controlled runs, PPO on a six-node multi-echelon supply chain (node 1 fixed demand, nodes 2–6 learning). Binary collapse criterion frozen before the main sweeps. Critical points from logistic regression of collapse on $w$; uncertainty from run-level bootstrap, $n = 2{,}000$, clustered by seed; Wilson intervals on cell fractions. The λ-sweep uses $\alpha = \beta = 0.88$, so its $\lambda = 1$ baseline ($w_{\mathrm{crit}} = 0.586$) differs from the null model's $0.685$ — that difference is the curvature effect, isolated by design, not a discrepancy. At $\lambda = 7$ the logistic is quasi-separated; we report the bootstrap critical point and suppress the slope. The 600k-step horizon estimate is reported only as a bound ($w_{\mathrm{crit}} < 0.50$) because the w-grid bracketed the transition poorly. All raw run CSVs, pre-registrations, and analysis scripts are in the project archive; every number in this post traces to a file.8

1. The phase-diagram framing is doing real work, not decoration: sharp threshold, bistability, metastability near the boundary, and parameter-dependent critical points are the defining phenomenology of a first-order-like transition, and each has a measured counterpart here.

2. Collapse ≔ terminal mean order rate below the pre-registered floor with the safe-policy profit signature (−128.1). Defined in docs/collapse_definition.md before Exp A ran; never amended.

3. The raw 600k logistic returns an unphysical point estimate (−0.60) because the grid lies almost entirely above the transition; the honest statement is the bound. Narrow grids are how you accidentally manufacture precision.

4. The curvature effect's CI, [−0.172, −0.033], excludes zero from above — significant and beneficial. Diminishing sensitivity compresses the perceived cost asymmetry of cooperating. Behavioral bias is not uniformly an enemy of coordination; that intuition dies here.

5. Rigor contract, explicitly: the propositions are theorems about the stated game and dynamics, complete with proofs; their bearing on PPO-trained neural agents is an empirical claim tested in Figs. 5–6. No theorem about deep MARL is being smuggled.

6. Weighted least squares with σ from bootstrap CI half-widths; χ² thresholds at the 95% level with appropriate degrees of freedom. Three-or-more-parameter laws fit three points exactly and are reported but not ranked — flexibility is not evidence.

7. The log law misses the λ=3 interval by 3×10⁻⁴. We reject at the stated rule and note the margin — and note also that re-running the bootstrap with a different seed can flip this verdict. Treat the log law as on probation, not executed; the dense sweep will not be kind to one of us.

8. Replication materials: pre-registrations (expB_preregistration.md, exp0b_preregistration.md), raw data (docs/*/.csv), frozen analysis pipeline (analyze_expA.py lineage), and the law-fitting script shipped with this post.
