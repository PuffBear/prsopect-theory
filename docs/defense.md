# Reviewer Response Defense

## 1. "Did you just tune it poorly?" 
**Reviewer Comment:** Existing literature has successfully applied RL and even PPO to supply chain synchronization and inventory management... Reviewers will demand proof that your failure is a fundamental mathematical limitation of continuous PPO, rather than just suboptimal hyperparameter tuning or reward shaping.

**Defense:**
Our findings demonstrate a fundamental mathematical limitation of continuous PPO in this domain, not a failure of tuning. We conducted exhaustive hyperparameter sweeps (including learning rates, entropy coefficients, clip ratios, and batch sizes) and experimented with extensive reward shaping, yet the agents still collapsed. The core issue lies in the fact that continuous PPO relies on a Gaussian policy distribution. Optimal inventory policies, such as the widely proven Base-Stock $(s,S)$ policy, are highly non-linear and zero-inflated—they require ordering exactly zero most of the time when inventory is above $s$, and a specific positive amount otherwise. A Gaussian distribution mathematically struggles to reliably output exact boundaries (like zero) without collapsing its variance, leading to either constant policy churn (noise) or premature convergence to sub-optimal local minima (e.g., ordering a constant average amount). This structural mismatch between the continuous action space parameterization and the discontinuous nature of optimal supply chain policies cannot be resolved simply through hyperparameter tuning.

## 2. Contradicting Existing SOTA
**Reviewer Comment:** There is a robust body of literature successfully using multi-agent deep RL for multi-echelon inventory management... Because your findings contradict the broader optimism in this sub-field, the burden of proof is heavily on you to explain why your agents collapsed while others report success.

**Defense:**
While we acknowledge the optimism and successes in the broader sub-field, a closer examination of these SOTA works reveals critical differences in problem formulation. Many successful applications of PPO or MARL in inventory management bypass the fundamental limitations we highlight by either: (1) heavily discretizing the action space, (2) using domain-specific action masking or custom distributions, or (3) restructuring the action itself (e.g., outputting a target stock level rather than a direct continuous order quantity). Our study deliberately applies standard continuous PPO to the raw problem of continuous order quantities to expose the underlying instability. The failures we observe—such as the RL-induced bullwhip effect—are masked in other papers by these specific architectural workarounds. Our findings do not invalidate prior success; rather, they rigorously explain *why* such workarounds were necessary in the first place, challenging the assumption that vanilla continuous RL algorithms are naturally suited for supply chain dynamics.

## 3. Lack of a Proposed Solution
**Reviewer Comment:** Diagnosing the disease is great, but top-tier venues usually want the cure. You mention that the solution requires "discrete-action algorithms or structured Base-Stock $(s,S)$ policy parameterizations," but you haven't implemented them in this draft.

**Defense:**
We firmly believe that thoroughly diagnosing a pervasive but poorly understood "disease" is a critical scientific contribution in itself. The primary objective of this paper is to serve as a rigorous mathematical autopsy of continuous PPO's failure modes in inventory dynamics. The "cure"—developing and rigorously benchmarking a custom structured Base-Stock $(s,S)$ policy parameterization or a novel discrete-action framework—is a substantial undertaking that warrants its own dedicated, comprehensive study. By deeply investigating the failure mechanics, such as how policy variance interacts with lead times to exponentially amplify the bullwhip effect, we establish the theoretical foundation necessary for the community to design these specialized solutions. Attempting to tack on a novel architectural solution in this manuscript would dilute the rigorous diagnostic focus of the paper. However, to address this feedback, we will include a proof-of-concept discrete action baseline in the appendix to empirically validate our proposed direction.
