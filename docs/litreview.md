# **Advanced Reinforcement Learning Paradigms in Multi-Echelon Supply Chains and Multi-Agent Systems: A Decade in Review (2015–2026)**

The intersection of Operations Research (OR) and advanced machine learning has catalyzed a fundamental paradigm shift in the management of complex, multi-echelon supply chains and decentralized multi-agent systems. Classical analytical methods, heavily reliant on dynamic programming, are notoriously vulnerable to the "triple curse of dimensionality" when state spaces, action spaces, and exogenous stochastic variables scale.1 While heuristic frameworks such as base-stock ![][image1] policies have historically dominated the domain, the advent of Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has provided a data-driven mechanism to bypass the intractable nature of exact analytical solutions.3 However, the transition from highly stylized continuous-control environments (e.g., standard robotic simulations) to the mathematically rigorous, constraint-bound realities of inventory control has exposed severe architectural limitations in conventional DRL algorithms.4  
This comprehensive analysis investigates the evolution of DRL and Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) methodologies between 2015 and 2026\. The investigation meticulously dissects the pathologies associated with continuous Gaussian action distributions in boundary-constrained and zero-inflated environments, the phenomena of policy collapse and lazy equilibria in multi-agent credit assignment, and the emergence of hybrid architectures that integrate classical OR structural approximations directly into neural parameterizations.6

## **The Pathology of Continuous Control in Inventory Reinforcement Learning**

The foundational algorithms driving the current wave of continuous-action DRL, such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), Soft Actor-Critic (SAC), and Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C), predominantly assume that the optimal policy can be parameterized as a Gaussian distribution. In these frameworks, the neural network outputs a mean ![][image2] and a standard deviation ![][image3], from which an action ![][image4] is sampled. While this mechanism is highly effective in unbounded or loosely bounded robotic kinematics, it introduces fatal mathematical and practical pathologies when applied to inventory replenishment, resource allocation, and market-making, where action spaces are stringently bounded and optimal policies frequently exhibit zero-inflation.8

### **Boundary Constraints and the Gaussian Clipping Pathology**

In supply chain operations, an action ![][image4] typically represents an order quantity or an inventory allocation along a specific network edge. This variable is fundamentally constrained by physical realities: it must be strictly non-negative (![][image5]) and is invariably capped by a maximum capacity constraint (![][image6]), dictated by supplier limitations, transportation bandwidth, or warehouse volumetric capacity.1  
When a standard Gaussian policy ![][image7] is deployed in this environment, the infinite support of the normal distribution dictates that probability mass will inevitably spill beyond the feasible domain ![][image8]. The conventional, naive workaround employed in standard continuous control is to sample from the Gaussian and subsequently apply a clipping function to force the action into the valid range. However, clipping generates a Dirac delta mass at the boundaries. If the neural network determines that the optimal action lies at or near the boundary, it will shift ![][image2] toward ![][image6] or ![][image9]. Consequently, a massive proportion of the sampled distribution falls outside the bounds and is clipped to the exact boundary value. This destroys the smoothness of the policy gradient, leading to biased, near-zero gradient estimates—a phenomenon known as boundary accumulation.11 The policy becomes trapped, unable to efficiently explore the interior of the action space, resulting in premature convergence to highly suboptimal behaviors.11  
To circumvent this mathematical artifact, sophisticated bounded parameterizations have been developed. The use of Scaled Beta distributions has proven exceptionally effective in these contexts.9 By parameterizing the action as a Beta distribution, where the neural network outputs the strictly positive shape parameters ![][image10] and ![][image11], the policy's support is naturally and strictly confined to the interval $$, which is then linearly scaled to ![][image8].9 The probability density function of the Beta distribution vanishes smoothly at the boundaries rather than accumulating mass. This guarantees that the policy can heavily skew volume toward either extreme—such as maximizing an order to fill a warehouse—without losing gradient information or introducing clipping bias.12  
Alternatively, architectures have integrated explicit, differentiable projection operators known as Feasibility Enforcement Layers (FELs) directly into the neural topology. In multi-echelon networks where a node must distribute inventory across multiple downstream edges, FELs apply a differentiable mathematical projection to the intermediate neural outputs, ensuring that the sum of the allocated quantities exactly equals or is strictly less than the available on-hand inventory.1 This guarantees continuous feasibility without the destructive effects of post-hoc Gaussian clipping.14

### **Zero-Inflation and Sparse Optimal Thresholds**

Beyond boundary constraints, continuous Gaussian policies suffer a critical failure in modeling zero-inflated optimal action spaces. In classical inventory theory, the optimal solution to numerous periodic-review systems—particularly those with non-zero fixed ordering costs—is characterized by an ![][image1] base-stock policy. Under this policy, the optimal action is precisely zero if the inventory position remains above the reorder point ![][image12], and a strictly positive order up to level ![][image13] if it drops below ![][image12].3  
A Gaussian policy, or any standard continuous distribution, struggles fundamentally to output an exact zero with high consistency. The inherent stochastic noise injected during exploration and execution means the policy will frequently generate infinitesimal, non-zero quantities (e.g., ![][image14]). In a robotic simulation, a negligible torque command has minimal consequence. In a supply chain environment, however, executing a continuous stream of "micro-orders" incurs massive fixed transactional, transportation, and administrative costs, completely destroying the economic viability of the policy and violating the fundamental structure of the ![][image1] heuristic.15  
To resolve this zero-inflation pathology, researchers have turned to hybrid continuous-discrete architectures and hurdle models.16 These models explicitly decouple the ordering decision into a hierarchical, two-stage process. The architecture first utilizes a latent Bernoulli component to model the discrete probability of ordering (representing the threshold ![][image12]). If the Bernoulli variable triggers a positive action, a separate continuous component—often modeled via a bounded Beta or a zero-inflated Negative Binomial/Poisson distribution—determines the exact magnitude of the order.17 This enables the policy network to safely collapse the order probability to absolute zero when the inventory level is sufficient, successfully mimicking the sparse thresholding required for optimal economic performance.18

## **Decentralization and MARL Dynamics: The "Lazy Agent" and Policy Collapse**

As inventory optimization models have scaled from single-node warehouses to complex, decentralized multi-echelon networks, the field has increasingly modeled these systems as Partially Observable Markov Games (POMGs) solved via Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL).19 The standard operational paradigm in this domain is Centralized Training with Decentralized Execution (CTDE), utilizing value decomposition networks (e.g., QMIX) or multi-agent policy gradients (e.g., MAPPO).20 However, MARL architectures deployed in cooperative supply chain environments are highly vulnerable to catastrophic policy collapse when guided by sparse, global team rewards.

### **Relative Overgeneralization and Sub-Optimal Nash Equilibria**

In a fully cooperative multi-agent environment—such as a decentralized multi-echelon supply chain striving to minimize total network cost—the reward signal is often global. A single, shared reward obscures the individual contribution of any specific agent. The action of one agent is heavily convoluted by the simultaneous exploratory actions of all other agents, a phenomenon recognized as cross-agent noise, where the variance of the gradient estimates scales proportionally with the number of agents ![][image15].22  
Value decomposition methods rely on the assumption that the joint action-value function can be monotonically factored into individual utility functions. In highly stochastic environments with sparse rewards, this assumption breaks down, leading to the pathology of relative overgeneralization.23 During training, an agent may execute an optimal action, but because its peers randomly execute poor actions, the resulting global reward is negative. The agent subsequently learns to suppress its optimal behavior. Ultimately, the network converges to a sub-optimal, non-cooperative Nash equilibrium where agents learn highly conservative, risk-averse policies.23 In supply chain terms, this often manifests as massive inventory hoarding at upstream nodes to prevent downstream stockouts, drastically inflating holding costs, or alternatively, a complete cessation of ordering to minimize holding costs, resulting in catastrophic lost sales.

### **Causal Credit Assignment and the Lazy Agent Pathology**

A specific sub-class of relative overgeneralization is the "lazy agent" problem. A lazy agent is an entity within a MARL system that completely ceases to participate in the cooperative effort, shifting the entire operational burden onto a subset of over-active peers.21 In inventory models, this may occur when one distribution center handles all fulfillment while others sit idle, or when upstream nodes refuse to replenish downstream nodes.  
Recent advancements have mathematically formalized the lazy agent using causal inference models. An agent is defined as fully lazy if and only if its actions exert a zero causal treatment effect on the external state transitions of the environment.24 Standard MARL architectures like QMIX inherently fail to detect this laziness because the aggregated global reward does not penalize individual inactivity as long as the active agents manage to achieve marginal success.21  
To rectify multi-agent credit assignment failures, architectures such as Lazy Agents Avoidance through Influencing External States (LAIES) integrate Pearl’s do-calculus to construct explicit counterfactual reasoning models.24 By estimating the hypothetical state of the environment had an agent chosen a different action, these models generate robust intrinsic motivation signals. Two primary signals have emerged: Individual Diligence Intrinsic (IDI) motivation, which rewards an agent strictly for actions that uniquely alter the external state, and Collaborative Diligence Intrinsic (CDI) motivation, which assesses the joint causal influence of localized sub-teams.27  
Similarly, in hierarchical and sequential environments, frameworks like Sequential Credit Assignment (SeCA) have been introduced to explicitly factorize complex joint advantage functions.28 SeCA dynamically orders agent execution based on their current marginal contribution to the team objective. By evaluating agents sequentially rather than simultaneously, SeCA isolates precise individual utilities, breaking the symmetry that causes relative overgeneralization and ensuring that all agents remain active and optimizing toward the global minimum cost.28

## **Architecting Deep RL for Multi-Echelon Supply Chains**

Multi-echelon inventory optimization (MEIO) poses exceptional challenges for reinforcement learning. The complex interplay of cascaded demand distortion (the bullwhip effect), highly variable transportation lead times, and the combinatorial explosion of the pipeline state space renders standard algorithms computationally intractable.15

### **Deep Controlled Learning and Variance Reduction**

Traditional actor-critic algorithms like A3C have been tested extensively against classical heuristics in lost-sales inventory settings. While they prove capable of matching general performance, they suffer from extreme variance, requiring immense computational overhead to stabilize.30 Furthermore, standard DRL frequently struggles to consistently outperform highly optimized heuristic baselines, such as the capped base-stock policy, due to the extreme noise inherent in stochastic demand and random lead times.32  
To bridge this performance gap, researchers have developed Deep Controlled Learning (DCL), an algorithm explicitly engineered for highly stochastic, input-driven Markov Decision Processes.32 Rather than relying on a continuously updated, highly volatile neural critic network to estimate action values, DCL reformulates the problem into an approximate policy iteration framework driven by rigorous simulation. The algorithm employs Classification-Based Policy Iteration (CBPI), treating policy extraction as a supervised classification task rather than a regression problem.32  
To conquer the variance injected by stochastic demand, DCL utilizes Common Random Numbers (CRN). During the evaluation of potential actions, CRN forces the simulation to expose every action to the exact same sequence of realized exogenous demand and lead-time shocks.32 This isolation guarantees that any difference in outcome is directly attributable to the action itself, rather than environmental noise. By coupling CRN with Sequential Halving (SH)—a bandit algorithm that dynamically allocates computational resources to the most promising optimal actions—DCL dramatically reduces required simulation effort. In complex environments, DCL consistently achieves optimality gaps of under 0.2%, representing an order-of-magnitude improvement over standard A3C architectures, which typically stall at 3-6% optimality gaps.32

### **Topological Inductive Biases and Iterative MARL**

The scalability of supply chain RL relies heavily on integrating the physical network topology directly into the neural architecture. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) provide a powerful inductive bias for encoding the structure of MEIO systems.14 By mapping warehouses, distribution centers, and retailers to network nodes, and modeling transportation routes as edges, GNN-parameterized policies ensure that the agent natively understands the downstream and upstream dependencies of its actions.14  
However, the fusion of MARL and GNNs introduces significant non-stationarity. If all nodes in a multi-echelon network update their replenishment policies simultaneously, the incoming inventory and outgoing demand for any single node become chaotic moving targets, preventing convergence.37 Iterative Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (IMARL) resolves this by implementing a phase-locked, sequential training regimen. Instead of concurrent updates, IMARL sequentially freezes the policies of upstream agents while downstream agents converge to local optima, iteratively sweeping through the network. This mechanism mitigates non-stationarity, providing stable targets and achieving vastly superior scalability across highly divergent, real-world network topographies.2

## **Bridging the Gap: Deep RL Parameterization of Base-Stock ![][image1] Policies**

Despite the allure of end-to-end continuous control, Operations Research has repeatedly proven that optimal policies for inventory management inherently follow specific mathematical structures. In systems with proportional holding and penalty costs, optimal policies often resemble base-stock or ![][image1] policies.3 Forcing a reinforcement learning agent to learn raw continuous order quantities in an unconstrained vacuum forces the agent to waste immense computational budget re-learning basic structural principles.15

### **Structured Action Spaces and Target Inventory Parameterization**

State-of-the-art DRL implementations have abandoned raw action outputs in favor of structured action representations. Instead of the neural network outputting an exact continuous order quantity, the architecture is designed such that the network outputs a parameterized target inventory position or target base-stock level.8  
In this continuous action representation, the actual executed action is deterministically computed outside the neural network. The algorithm calculates the difference between the network's outputted target level and the current on-hand plus pipeline inventory.4 This structured parameterization massively reduces the necessary exploration manifold. By effectively turning the RL agent into a dynamic parameter-tuner for a generalized base-stock policy, the agent naturally recovers near-optimal behavior in a fraction of the time required by raw continuous actors, entirely bypassing the curse of dimensionality associated with high-resolution discrete binning in multi-product systems.4

### **Structure-Informed Policy Networks and Analytical Regularization**

To guarantee mathematical robustness, particularly when the agent encounters out-of-distribution (OOD) states rarely visited during training, modern frameworks integrate classical OR theory directly into the deep learning optimization process. Structure-Informed Deep Reinforcement Learning embeds known theoretical constraints—such as ![][image16]\-convexity and state-variable monotonicity—directly into the neural network's loss function.8  
Through differential regularization, the training process explicitly computes the gradients of the policy outputs with respect to the endogenous state variables.8 If the neural policy attempts to violate fundamental structural truths—for example, if it commands a higher replenishment order when current inventory is extremely high compared to when it is low (a severe violation of monotonicity)—the structure-informed regularizer applies a massive numerical penalty to the loss function.8 This sophisticated constraint mechanism restricts the neural network's exploration space to the subset of theoretically viable policies. By weaving the analytical rigor of operations research into the data-driven flexibility of deep reinforcement learning, these architectures ensure that learned policies remain fundamentally reliable, theoretically consistent, and highly robust to sudden demand shocks or environmental non-stationarity.8

### **Deep Controlled Learning and Variance Suppression in Stochastic Inventory**

Traditional deep reinforcement learning methodologies repeatedly fail to surpass the performance of simple, highly optimized heuristics in lost-sales inventory settings. Temizöz et al. (2025) provide a definitive solution to this problem through the introduction of Deep Controlled Learning (DCL). The authors recognized that the primary reason continuous actor-critic algorithms like A3C collapse or stagnate in these environments is the extreme variance introduced by exogenous demand and stochastic lead times. The neural critic network simply cannot accurately regress a continuous value function through the noise. By completely discarding continuous Gaussian regression in favor of treating the optimal action selection as a discrete, supervised classification problem (Classification-Based Policy Iteration), DCL bypasses gradient bias. Furthermore, the integration of Common Random Numbers guarantees that every action is evaluated against identical demand simulations, crushing variance. Consequently, DCL manages to achieve optimality gaps of under 0.2%, proving that DRL can indeed consistently beat state-of-the-art heuristics when mathematical stochasticity is properly managed.32

### **The General-Purpose Viability of Deep Reinforcement Learning**

Prior to the integration of specific inductive biases and structural regularizers, the operations research community required foundational proof that general-purpose DRL could solve intractable inventory problems. Gijsbrechts et al. (2022) provided this landmark evidence by modeling highly complex scenarios—such as dual-sourcing with differing lead times and multi-echelon networks—as Markov Decision Processes solved via the Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) algorithm. The study is pivotal because it demonstrates that, even without knowing the underlying structural properties of the optimal policy (which are analytically intractable for these environments), a raw DRL agent can iteratively learn a policy that matches or slightly exceeds the performance of highly sophisticated, problem-specific heuristics. However, the study also openly acknowledges the severe computational burden and massive hyperparameter tuning times required to stabilize the continuous A3C algorithm, highlighting the need for the more structured approaches that would follow in subsequent years.30

### **Topological Inductive Biases and Differentiable Projections**

As supply chain models expanded from serial nodes to decentralized graphs, the limitations of standard fully connected neural networks became apparent. Alvo et al. (2023) directly address this by combining Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) with a novel optimization technique called Hindsight Differentiable Policy Optimization (HDPO). By embedding the physical supply chain graph into the GNN architecture, the policy inherently understands the upstream and downstream consequences of inventory flow. Furthermore, the authors conquer the continuous control boundary problem through the invention of Feasibility Enforcement Layers (FELs). Instead of a naive Gaussian clip that destroys gradient information, FELs mathematically project the neural network's unbounded allocation decisions onto the exact feasible polytope defined by the node's current on-hand inventory. This ensures that the agent never attempts to ship inventory it does not possess, maintaining perfect mathematical feasibility while allowing gradients to backpropagate cleanly through the projection operator, thus avoiding policy collapse.1

### **Causal Inference and the Diagnosis of Lazy Agents**

In environments requiring highly coordinated multi-agent interactions under a single shared reward, value decomposition techniques consistently fail. Liu et al. (2023) provide the definitive formalization of this failure through the lens of causal inference. The authors demonstrate that in standard MARL algorithms like QMIX, sparse rewards lead directly to relative overgeneralization, causing the emergence of "lazy agents." They mathematically define a lazy agent using Pearl's do-calculus as an agent whose action exerts absolute zero causal treatment effect on the external state transitions of the environment. Because the global reward does not isolate this failure, the lazy agent free-rides on the efforts of its peers. To solve this, the authors introduce the LAIES framework, which generates counterfactual baseline models to compute the exact causal influence of each agent. By rewarding agents with Individual and Collaborative Diligence Intrinsic motivations, the framework forces all agents to actively manipulate the environment, entirely curing the lazy agent pathology and achieving state-of-the-art coordination.21

### **Mitigating Non-Stationarity in Multi-Echelon Iterative MARL**

When applying multi-agent reinforcement learning to complex, multi-echelon inventory systems, a critical failure point is environmental non-stationarity. If a retailer, a distribution center, and a factory all attempt to simultaneously update their RL-driven ordering policies, the state transition dynamics become chaotic. Ziegner et al. (2025) address this specific scaling failure by introducing Iterative Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (IMARL). Rather than relying on simultaneous decentralized execution, IMARL applies a sequential, phase-locked training regimen. By iteratively freezing the learning process of upstream supply chain nodes while downstream nodes converge, IMARL stabilizes the target distributions for the neural networks. This sequential locking allows the use of strictly continuous PPO actors without the networks violently diverging due to shifting demand patterns. The study validates this approach across an exhaustive matrix of 13 complexity scenarios, proving that IMARL is the key to scaling DRL into highly divergent, real-world supply chain topologies.2

### **Structure-Informed Regularization and Analytical Constraints**

The tension between the pure data-driven flexibility of deep reinforcement learning and the rigorous, proven mathematical constraints of operations research is reconciled by Maggiar et al. (2025). The authors recognize that while standard DRL agents can learn optimal policies in highly frequented state spaces, their behavior in rare, out-of-distribution states is often chaotic and theoretically unsound. To solve this, they introduce Structure-Informed Policy Networks. Instead of outputting raw actions, the neural network outputs parameterized base-stock target levels. Crucially, the training process explicitly calculates the gradient of these targets with respect to the environment's state variables. If the neural network attempts to violate proven OR principles—such as ![][image16]\-convexity or state monotonicity—the loss function is heavily penalized. This analytical regularization ensures that the neural policy always remains within a theoretically sound manifold, preventing out-of-distribution policy collapse and bridging the gap between deep learning and classical inventory mathematics.8

### **Scaling via Continuous Action Representations**

In multi-product inventory environments, the curse of dimensionality strikes the action space directly. If an agent must order 10 different products, and each product action is discretized into 100 bins, the network's output layer explodes into an intractable combinatorial space. Vanvuchelen et al. (2025) explicitly analyze this pathology, proving that transitioning to strictly continuous action representations is mathematically mandatory to scale DRL in these environments. By formulating the network to output a continuous vector rather than a categorical distribution over discrete bins, the authors demonstrate that continuous representations fundamentally bypass output layer explosion. The study provides critical empirical evidence that continuous actors require significantly less training time and computational overhead while perfectly matching the performance guarantees of state-of-the-art heuristic replenishment policies.4

### **Sequential Evaluation for Explicable Credit Assignment**

Addressing the relative overgeneralization problem from a temporal perspective, Zang et al. (2023) introduce the Sequential Credit Assignment (SeCA) framework. The authors note that standard value decomposition networks implicitly and inexplicably fracture the shared reward, providing no direct guidance on individual policy optimization. This causes agents to fall into sub-optimal lazy traps because they cannot discern the exact value of their contribution. SeCA solves this by factorizing the complex joint interaction into a strictly sequential evaluation process. By dynamically ordering the agents and evaluating their marginal contribution to the team reward one by one, the framework provides exact, explicable credit assignment. This breaks the simultaneous action symmetry that causes policy collapse, ensuring highly precise coordination in complex tasks.28

### **Bounded Parameterizations via Scaled Beta Policies**

The application of DRL to order-driven markets and financial supply chains presents similar boundary pathologies to inventory control. Jerome et al. (2022) provide a definitive solution by completely replacing standard Gaussian distributions with dynamically controlled Scaled Beta distributions. In market making, an agent must distribute order volume across a contiguous price interval. A standard Gaussian policy fails because clipping distorts the volume allocation. By utilizing a Beta distribution where the deep reinforcement learning algorithm controls the ![][image10] and ![][image11] shape parameters, the probability density is perfectly confined within the hard boundaries. This allows the RL agent to dynamically skew the volume distribution left or right based on real-time inventory risk, maintaining perfect mathematical feasibility without gradient loss.9

### **Hierarchical RL for Omnichannel Capacity Constraints**

In modern omnichannel supply chains, agents must manage disparate decision frequencies—such as weekly replenishment orders versus daily fulfillment and lateral transshipment operations. Standard DRL policies collapse when attempting to optimize across these varying temporal scales simultaneously. To solve this, MDPI (2022) details a Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) architecture. By explicitly separating the decision timing into a high-level weekly actor and a low-level daily actor, the HRL framework mitigates the curse of dimensionality and prevents policy dilution. Furthermore, to address severe zero-inflation and demand spikes caused by Merton-type jump-diffusion shocks, the architecture integrates a capacity-aware state-action encoding mechanism. This compresses the control interface, ensuring that the bounded stochastic policies never violate hard capacity limits during volatile shock events.43

## **Conclusion**

The trajectory of deep reinforcement learning in operations research has shifted dramatically from generic algorithmic application to the development of highly specialized, domain-aware architectures. The mathematical limitations of standard Gaussian policies have been successfully navigated through the deployment of bounded Beta parameterizations, hurdle models, and differentiable feasibility layers, completely eradicating the destructive artifacts of boundary clipping and continuous zero-inflation. Concurrently, the vulnerability of decentralized supply chain networks to "lazy agent" pathologies and relative overgeneralization has catalyzed the integration of causal inference and sequential evaluation, ensuring mathematically rigorous credit assignment in sparse-reward environments. Ultimately, the most profound advancements have emerged from hybrid models that directly fuse the structural proofs of classical operations research with neural architectures. By leveraging structured base-stock parameterizations and ![][image16]\-convexity regularizers, modern DRL frameworks achieve unprecedented scalability, stability, and reliability, rendering them fully viable for the extreme stochasticity of real-world multi-echelon supply chains.
